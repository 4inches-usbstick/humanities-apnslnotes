Lemon v KurtzmanMarch 3 - June 28, 1971Pennsylvania and Rhode Island passed laws to pay teachers of secular subjects in religious schools with state funds, in the late 1960's. The appellants in this case represented tax payers and citizens who believed that the statute violated the separation of church and state described in the First Amendment. The district court granted the state officials' motion to dismiss the case.Do statutes that provide state funding for non-public, non-secular schools violate the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment?8-1 in favor to Lemon as to Pennsylvania's statute
8-0 in favor to Lemon as to Rhode Island's statute		The Pennyslvania and Rhode Island statutes are unconstitutional under the religion clause of the First Amendment because of the entanglement of state and church. The Court held that the statutes failed a three-pronged test (later to be known as the Lemon Test), which helped determine that the statutes constituted an excessive government entanglement with religion.

Pottawatomie v EarlsMarch 19 - June 27, 2002The Student Activities Drug Testing Policy adopted by the Tecumseh, Oklahoma School District requires all middle and high school students to consent to urinalysis testing for drugs in order to participate in any extracurricular activity. Two students and their parents brought suit, alleging that the policy violates the Fourth Amendment. The District Court granted the School District summary judgment, but the Court of Appeals held that the policy violated the Fourth Amendment. The appellate court concluded that before imposing a suspicionless drug-testing program a school must demonstrate some identifiable drug abuse problem among a sufficient number of those tested, such that testing that group will actually redress its drug problem, which the School District had failed to demonstrate.Is the Student Activities Drug Testing Policy, which requires all students who participate in competitive extracurricular activities to submit to drug testing, consistent with the Fourth Amendment?5–4 Decision for PottawatomieThe Court held that, because the policy reasonably serves the School District's important interest in preventing drug use among its students, it is constitutional. The Court reasoned that the Board's method of obtaining samples and maintaining test results was also minimally intrusive on the students' limited privacy interest.

Dec 1, 2006
Argued
Mar 19, 2007
Decided:
Jun 25, 2007		After student Joseph Fredrick got suspended after holding up a banner with the message "Bong Hits 4 Jesus" a slang reference to illegal smoking, he sued under 42 U.S.C. 1983, the federal civil rights statute, alleging a violation of his First Amendment right to freedom of speech. The district court had already ruled in favor of Morse, claiming that there was no constitutional violation but once it reached the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit they reserved the ruling. The Ninth District claimed that since Frederick was punished for his message rather than for any disturbance, which went against the decision made in Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District, which had extended the First Amendment protection to student speech except where the speech would cause a disturbance, the punishment was unconstitutional.Does the First Amendment allow public schools to prohibit students from displaying messages promoting the use of illegal drugs at school-supervised events?

Does a school official have qualified immunity from a damages lawsuit under 42 U.S.C. 1983 when, in accordance with school policy, she disciplines a student for displaying a banner with a drug reference at a school-supervised event?		5–4 Decision for Principal MorseThe Supreme Court reserved the decision made by the Ninth Circut Court and ruled that school officials can prohibit students from displaying messages that promote illegal drug use, therefore making Fredrick's punishment fair. The reason behind this decision was seen in Chief Justice John Roberts's majority opinion, which held that "although students do have some right to political speech even while in school, that this right does not extend to pro-drug messages that may undermine the school's important mission to discourage drug use." With this decision, the court also affirmed that the standard set by Tinker would not always be applied.
